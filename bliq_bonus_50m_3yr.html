€50M & 3 years — a different shape, not a smaller system

What I would build to launch a commercially viable driverless service on €50M in 3 years, set against what the technically-best system would do, across all six dimensions. Highlighted text marks the decisive divergences and the concrete choices (named hardware, counts, costs).

★ Core thesis: the technically-best system wants to handle the city; the €50M-in-3-years system picks a route the city cannot easily say no to. The economics only work if the ODD is restricted enough that the AI problem becomes tractable in ~18 months instead of ~5 years.
Technically-best (handle the city) €50M / 3yr (win a corridor)
🧱Architecture
Technically-best system
  • General urban L4: custom perception + novel prediction, built to scale to the whole city
  • Foundation model on a path to the critical loop (longer-term)
  • Maximum in-house capability; minimise external dependencies you can't tune
vs
€50M · 3 yr — what I’d do
  • Hybrid stack: learned perception → classical, RSS-constrained planner; heavy Autoware reuse
  • No foundation model on the critical path — VLAs offline only (auto-labelling, scenario mining)
  • Trade vehicle capability for fixed-route infrastructure: HD maps + V2X beacons at the worst intersections
  • Single Orin Nano-class SoC + safety MCU (the MCU path) — redundancy without a second big SoC
📍ODD
Technically-best system
  • Open urban driving, city-wide, all roads
  • Up to 50–60 km/h
  • Broad weather + day/night ambition
vs
€50M · 3 yr — what I’d do
  • Fixed / near-fixed corridors the city can't easily refuse (tech-park→U-Bahn, airport, hospital, port loop)
  • ≤50 km/h — keeps ISO 22737 (LSAD) framing close and shrinks braking-distance / MRC arguments
  • Geofenced ~5–10 km²; dry, daylight, weekday off-peak windows only
📡Sensors
Technically-best system
  • <mark>LiDAR: 4× Hesai AT128-class 360° long-range (200 m), à la Pony.ai 7th-gen</mark>
  • Radar: 6× 4D imaging radar (front + 4 corner + rear), full overlap
  • 13+ HD cameras for 360° overlapping coverage up to 500 m (Waymo-style)
  • Maximum range + redundancy; cost secondary to capability
vs
€50M · 3 yr — what I’d do
  • LiDAR: 1× AT128-class forward (mid-range) + 3× QT128-class short-range surround — fewer, cheaper, ODD-matched
  • Radar: 5× 4D imaging (1 front + 4 corner) — enough for 360° at ≤50 km/h, no rear-long-range
  • 8× HD cameras — surround + traffic-light/sign; no highway-range tele stereo
  • Per-vehicle sensor+compute BOM ~€14–28k; range relaxed by the ≤50 km/h limit
👥Team
Technically-best system
  • ~120 engineers at steady state
  • Deep subdomain specialists across the full stack
  • Built for the hardest problem
vs
€50M · 3 yr — what I’d do
  • ~40 engineers, deep generalists @ €65–75k loaded (Berlin/EU)
  • 10 AI/ML · 6 vehicle integration · 6 remote-ops · 5 sim/data · 4 safety · 3 mapping · 4 backend · 2 product
  • Narrower individual responsibilities accepted; hire people who span vehicle + autonomy + safety
✅Validation
Technically-best system
  • General urban scenario coverage; order-of-magnitude more validation miles
  • Large bespoke simulation + custom SiL pipeline across the whole ODD
  • Statistical safety case over open driving
vs
€50M · 3 yr — what I’d do
  • Route-specific coverage — every intersection on the route characterised
  • SiL/sim tooling ≈ €9M line: NVIDIA DRIVE Sim/Omniverse licences, FM-training cluster, log-replay + data pipeline
  • Real miles concentrated on short, repeated routes (cheap to accumulate)
  • Shadow-mode disagreement logging from day one feeds the retrain loop
💶Business
Technically-best system
  • Competes with Uber / open ride-hail
  • Long-term option to become Waymo
  • CAC-driven growth; software-vendor optionality
vs
€50M · 3 yr — what I’d do
  • Competes with a specific mediocre commute/shuttle, not the cheapest car on the road
  • Different willingness-to-pay: passengers compare to a fixed alternative
  • Supervisor ratio ~1:3 accepted as permanent — “AI-assisted,” not “almost autonomous”
  • Sacrifice: the option to become Waymo — a beachhead business, harder to expand into open urban L4 later

At EU engineer rates (~€8.4M for 40 engineers over 3 years), this validation/SiL/compute line is the single largest spend — which is the point: for a small team, money buys validation throughput, not headcount or vehicles.

What gets sacrificed: the long-term option to become Waymo. The fixed-route shape is a genuinely different business — a useful beachhead, but harder to expand into open urban operations later than if you’d built for the harder problem from the start. The technically-best company can become a shuttle operator; the shuttle operator struggles to become the technically-best company, because its architecture and team are wrong for it. Given €50M and 3 years, I’d still choose this — that budget is not enough for general urban L4, and pretending otherwise just buys a slower failure.
