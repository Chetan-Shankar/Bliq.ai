Build vs Buy — can Bliq just license perception (e.g. Autobrains)?

Autobrains is a real, well-funded (~$140M) Israeli vendor that licenses self-learning, signature-based perception software to OEMs and Tier-1 suppliers. Its pitch — far less labelling, ~10× less compute, strong edge-case behaviour, explainable — is genuinely attractive to a small team. But three structural facts decide it for Bliq:

  • The economics are OEM-scale. Per-vehicle BOM and compute savings only dominate across millions of units (their own business case is built on ~1M-car programmes). At Bliq's fleet of dozens, per-vehicle savings are nearly irrelevant.
  • It's perception only. Licensing Autobrains gives you a perception module — not the planner, safety stack, ODD manager, remote-assist layer or the L4 robotaxi integration Bliq still has to own.
  • It cedes the differentiator. A closed third-party perception black box breaks the supervisor-intervention data flywheel above — the very thing the rest of the architecture depends on for improvement and the safety case.

The cash picture (below) actually favours buying at Bliq's small scale — licensing avoids the heavy up-front engineering of an in-house perception team. That's real. The chart just can't show the strategic cost: lost IP, a broken flywheel, and a hard dependency on an external vendor's roadmap. And at scale the lines cross — per-vehicle licensing eventually exceeds the fixed in-house cost.

★ VERDICT — Feasible, but not as the core strategy. Reuse open-source perception (Autoware) and build the proprietary urban perception heads in-house (your differentiator + data flywheel). A vendor like Autobrains is best held as a de-risking fallback for the perception module if in-house perception slips — never the spine of the stack.
