Level 3 vs Level 4 — the four differences that matter

Comparison across technical architecture, validation, certification, and operational deployment. Highlighted text marks the load-bearing claims.

★ The spine of the whole comparison: who is the fallback. In L3 the human driver is the ultimate fallback; in L4 there is none inside the ODD — so every “explainability / explicit behaviour / explicit planner / redundancy” argument becomes strictly stronger for an L4 operator like Bliq.
🧩Technical architecture
LEVEL 3 — human is the fallback

Human driver is the fallback. The vehicle's job is to detect its own limits and hand back with enough lead time. Drive Pilot is built redundant (electrics, steering, braking doubled) but can always transfer the task back to the human.

Mercedes Drive Pilot: ~35 sensors, LiDAR essential, redundant by design — but a handback exists.

LEVEL 4 — the vehicle is the fallback

There is no human in the seat — the vehicle must execute the fallback (MRC) itself. The safety stack has to take control end-to-end, not just signal for help.

This single difference cascades through the whole architecture: more redundancy, dual compute paths, mandatory remote supervision, dramatically more SOTIF analysis.

L4 requires fault tolerance and the absence of single points of failure. Redundancy via duplicate sensors, overlapping coverage, or independent modalities (Waymo overlaps cameras/radar/LiDAR rather than blindly duplicating). The hard problems are compute, steering, braking, power — because there is no human fallback.

Mercedes Drive Pilot — redundant L3 design, 95 km/h (TÜV Rheinland / KBA)Waymo — overlapping multi-modal sensor redundancy (YooJung Ahn)ISO 26262 — fault tolerance / no single point of failure
📏Validation effort
LEVEL 3 — human is the fallback

Tight ODD: highway only, ≤95 km/h, structured environment. Far smaller scenario space, and a human safety net behind every edge case.

Drive Pilot ODD: right lane, flowing traffic, ~13,200 km of approved autobahn.

LEVEL 4 — the vehicle is the fallback

L4 needs roughly an order of magnitude more validation miles for an equivalent safety claim. The urban scenario space at 50 km/h is much richer than highway L3 and there is no human safety net.

Most of that validation cannot be replaced by simulation — the reg-relevant claim still needs real-world miles.

ISO 21448 (SOTIF) — scenario coverage & validation of intended functionWaymo — long-tail validation at scale (Anguelov)
📜Certification effort
LEVEL 3 — human is the fallback

Approved under UN R157 (ALKS) for the specific highway function. No technical-supervisor concept and no operating-area approval required.

World-first UN R157 system approval granted by KBA; sold as a consumer option.

LEVEL 4 — the vehicle is the fallback

L4 requires the EU 2022/1426 small-series pathway plus AFGBV — which add the technical-supervisor concept and operating-area approval that L3 does not need.

L4 is also more politically scrutinised: the absence of a driver makes incidents far more visible.

UN R157 (ALKS) — the L3 function approvalEU 2022/1426 — small-series L4 type approvalAFGBV — technical supervisor + operating-area approval
🚘Operational deployment
LEVEL 3 — human is the fallback

L3 vehicles are sold to consumers. The owner is present and is the legal fallback; the manufacturer ships a product and moves on.

Drive Pilot: a €5,950 option on S-Class / EQS, owner-operated.

LEVEL 4 — the vehicle is the fallback

L4 vehicles in Europe today are essentially fleet-only. The operational model is closer to airline operations than to car sales — continuous supervision, dispatch, maintenance. Bliq is doing the right thing by being an operator, not a software vendor.

Remote assistance substitutes for the L3 driver — which introduces a connectivity-loss failure mode L3 never has.
